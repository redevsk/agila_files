Background & Existing Systems
Vector control is increasingly adopting IoT sensors and GIS dashboards for proactive surveillance. Recent studies have built IoT-enabled ovitraps and traps that monitor environmental conditions and mosquito activity continuously. For example, the MOISS system deployed on Madeira Island uses solar-powered ovitrap sensors to log water pH, temperature, humidity, etc., every 15 minutes. Commercial “smart traps” like the Biogents BG-Counter use infrared sensors to automatically differentiate and count mosquitoes as they enter the trap, transmitting counts and local temp/humidity to the cloud. These high-tech traps eliminate much manual labor and can feed GIS dashboards in real time. Integrated public-health programs (e.g. Puerto Rico’s vector unit and Foz do Iguaçu’s VITEC system) now geotag thousands of traps and case data into dashboards for hot-spot mapping and task management. In short, the literature shows that continuous IoT monitoring and map-based platforms are feasible and improve early detection compared to reactive methods.

User Roles & Core MVP Features
Citizen (Public User) – No account needed to view maps of mosquito activity and interventions. A public GIS portal shows reported breeding sites, cleared areas, and a risk heatmap. (For example, ArcGIS dashboards have been used to share neighborhood-level mosquito data in Puerto Rico.) We allow citizen reports via login: a simple mobile/web app lets people drop a geo-tagged pin, attach photos, and submit a breeding-site report. Logged-in users can track report status (e.g. “under review”, “inspector assigned”). (Mobile reporting has proven valuable in other dengue programs.) The citizen view also provides public alerts or guidance (e.g. “clean water containers”) though that’s optional for MVP.

Barangay (Field Team) – This includes Field Inspectors and Treatment Teams at the barangay level. They use a mobile GIS app (offline-capable) for daily tasks. Features:

Inspection Workflow – Inspectors see assigned tasks on a map and list, including routine patrol routes and alerts from the system. Each task shows location, reason (citizen report or sensor alert), and due date. Inspectors can validate alerts: e.g. tap “open trap alert” or “report of larvae” and then record the finding. They fill out a form of findings (larvae/pupae present or not, count, container type, etc.) and attach geo-tagged photos. They then update site status (e.g. mark site as “breeding confirmed” or “false alarm”). Like the Brazilian VITEC system, the app can work offline and geocode on site. (Traps and ovitraps have unique IDs or QR codes so the app can log which device or site is being serviced.)
Ovitrap Maintenance – Smart ovitraps report their own status (battery, water level, etc.). The system flags traps needing service. Inspectors log trap maintenance in the app (change water, battery, etc.) and reset the maintenance counter. This prevents stale data (a key insight from MOISS was that solar/battery power must be managed for continuous sensing).
Treatment Workflow – When an inspector confirms a breeding site, a treatment task is generated. The Treatment Team app (which may be same or separate app) shows confirmed sites requiring action. Treatment personnel see location, recommended actions (cleanup, larvicide, source elimination). They document work by uploading before/after photos and marking each step done. Finally they mark the task complete, which triggers the site into the monitoring phase.
City/Municipality (Administrator) – This higher-level role oversees all barangays. Key features:

GIS Command Dashboard: An interactive city map with layers for citizen reports, confirmed sites, trap locations, inspection routes, treatment status, and cleared zones. Charts/summary stats (e.g. number of tasks, traps above threshold, average larvae counts) accompany the map. Users can filter by barangay, date, or status. (Studies note managers need dashboards combining maps and charts to allocate resources.)
Risk Heatmap & Analytics: The system computes a dynamic risk score for each area (see below) and displays it on the map. City staff can view historical trends (e.g. time series of trap catches or index charts). Heatmaps (similar to BG-Counter’s hotspot maps) instantly show where adult activity or reports are highest. Threshold settings can be configured (e.g. flag any trap exceeding X counts in a day) to auto-flag high-risk zones.
Task & Team Management: Administrators assign or reassign inspection and treatment tasks. This can be done directly from the dashboard (click a site to dispatch an inspector or crew). The platform can integrate with Workforce-style apps (as used in PR) so that new tasks appear on inspectors’ devices daily. Admins also manage sentinel devices (add/remove units, update locations) and monitor their health (battery, connectivity status).
Reporting & Sharing: The city can export reports (e.g. weekly summaries of findings, heatmaps, trap indexes). Data can be shared with health agencies or the public at an aggregated level. (Importantly, only aggregate spatial data is made public – private addresses or personal info are never exposed.)
Risk Scoring & Alerts
We propose a transparent composite risk score for each site or neighborhood, combining multiple indicators (as recommended in outbreak modeling). Factors include:

Entomological Activity: Trap catch or ovitrap index values. For example, a high 7-day rolling average of IR-detected insect entries or egg counts boosts risk. (BG-Counter style data can be binned by time of day or week to capture peaks.)
Citizen Reports: Recent validated reports of breeding. Each confirmed report (with photos) adds weight. Reports alone don’t prove infestation (inspectors validate), but multiple reports in an area raise the alert level.
Historical Sites: A past confirmed breeding site (in the last season) increases baseline risk. Sites remain “known” sources.
Trap/Device Status: If a sentinel unit reports low water level, low battery, or long time since maintenance, the site is temporarily flagged as uncertain (to prompt check). Conversely, a newly-serviced trap with no catch may temporarily lower risk.
Weather Conditions: Recent rainfall, high humidity or optimal temperature (from local or API data) increase breeding likelihood. For example, risk can scale up after heavy rain in the past week.
Treatment History: If an area was just treated with larvicide, immediate risk falls (subject to a decay factor). If no treatment occurred for a while despite other risk cues, risk stays high.
These inputs are combined (e.g. weighted sum or logistic model). For a simple MVP, we can normalize each factor (0–1) and compute:

RiskScore = 0.4×(trapIndex) + 0.2×(reportCount) + 0.1×(historicalPresence) + 0.2×(weatherFactor) + 0.1×(treatmentLagFactor).

The weights and thresholds would be tuned using historical data. For example, one study showed a composite dengue risk index strongly predicted outbreaks (areas with higher score had ~2.5× more cases). In practice, we define actionable thresholds: e.g. RiskScore>0.7 = High Risk (inspect this week), 0.4–0.7 = Medium (routine follow-up), below 0.4 = Low. Trap-specific thresholds can also trigger alerts (the BG system sets per-trap triggers for control measures).

Implementation & Operations
Devices & Connectivity: Sentinel units (smart ovitraps/traps) should use low-power wide-area networking (e.g. NB-IoT or LTE-M) or 3G/4G. Each unit stores data locally (e.g. on microSD) if offline, and syncs when reconnected. Solar panels (even small ones) are recommended to keep batteries charged for long-term deployment. A mobile module (with SIM) or LoRa radio can uplink data every 15 min to the cloud.
Cloud Platform: Use a reliable backend to ingest IoT data and serve the GIS. This could be ArcGIS Online or an open stack (PostGIS + Mapbox, etc.). The Risk Engine (a cloud function) processes incoming sensor and report data to update scores and layers. A REST API or WebSocket can push alerts to the dashboard and to mobile apps.
Mobile Apps: Inspectors and treatment crews use a mobile app (Android/iOS). This app allows offline geolocation (using GPS) and form entry. ArcGIS Survey123 or a custom app can be used (Puerto Rico used Esri Field Maps/Survey123 successfully). We include forms for “Inspect Ovitrap/Trap”, “Confirm Breeding”, and “Treatment Complete”. The app should scan trap IDs (QR/barcode) or allow manual entry to tie data to specific devices.
Data Privacy: Protect personal data rigorously. Citizen reports are tied only to location (no names). As in Foz do Iguaçu’s VITEC, we avoid storing any patient or resident identifiers in the public system. All reports/photos are linked only to grid locations or anonymized site codes. Map views aggregate data to neighborhood granularity when shared externally. Access to the dashboard should require LGU login, with role-based permissions (citizen vs. barangay vs. city). All data transfers must be encrypted (TLS) and user authentication enforced.
Workflow Integration: The platform automates the workflow shown in the design: sensors → alerts → inspection → (yes/no) → treatment → monitoring. In practice:
Continuous Monitoring: Sentinel devices send data to the command center. The Risk Engine updates scores and flags high-risk sites.
Inspection Assignment: The system generates inspection tasks (in GIS/Workforce) for flagged sites or citizen reports. Inspectors get notifications.
Field Validation: Inspectors record outcomes. A negative find simply closes the alert; a positive find escalates. All actions update the GIS status in real time.
Treatment Dispatch: When breeding is confirmed, the system creates a treatment work order. Treatment crews follow the map-guided task (cleanup, larvicide, source removal), documenting each step.
Post-Treatment Monitoring: The treated area enters a “monitor” state. Sentinel traps remain active. If no new activity is detected over the monitoring period, the system marks the area Cleared. If sensors or reports pick up new larvae, the area re-enters High Risk and the cycle repeats.
This workflow mirrors successful implementations: e.g. PRVCU uses GIS Workforce to assign weekly trap maintenance, and mobile apps to log field data. The integrated surveillance in Foz do Iguaçu (VITEC) showed that automating data flow greatly accelerates response—what took weeks manually can be done daily. By adhering to proven methods (mobile geocoding, unique trap IDs, risk indices) and existing IoT designs, the proposed MVP is technically sound and operationally viable.
